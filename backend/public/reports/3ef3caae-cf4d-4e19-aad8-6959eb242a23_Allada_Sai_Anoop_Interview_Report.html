What is a deadlock in operating systems?
Mentioned "switches logged in a loop" — vague and not technically accurate Could not explain the four necessary conditions: mutual exclusion, hold and wait, no preemption, circular wait
✗ Concept vaguely recalled — definition inaccurate, conditions not known
📚 What a Good Answer Looks Like
A deadlock is a state where two or more processes are each waiting for a resource held by another, creating a circular dependency that halts all of them indefinitely. Four necessary conditions (Coffman): (1) Mutual Exclusion — resource used by one at a time; (2) Hold and Wait — process holds one resource while waiting for another; (3) No Preemption — resources can't be forcibly taken; (4) Circular Wait — P1 waits on P2, P2 waits on P1.

Difference between TCP and UDP protocols

📚 What a Good Answer Looks Like
TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) is connection-oriented: establishes a 3-way handshake, guarantees ordered and reliable delivery, performs error checking and retransmission. Used for HTTP, file transfer, email. UDP (User Datagram Protocol) is connectionless: no handshake, no delivery guarantee, lower latency. Used for live video, gaming, DNS. Trade-off: TCP = reliability; UDP = speed.
